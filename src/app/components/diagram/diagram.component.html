Atrium

The atrium is certainly the most important part of the domus. Individuals of the higher classes put great effort into their atrium. It was the first thing guests would see and was an opportunity to make a statement. Within the atrium was generally a decorative water feature, the impluvium, and had an opening in the ceiling called the compluvium. Preserved domus show extravagant tile mosaics on the floors among other extravagant features. The atrium would also act as a place to display awards from both civic and military accolades.

Vestibulum

The vestibulum is the entrance corridor into the domus. In large part, the vestibulum was reserved for larger estates of the upper class. However, it was not uncommon for merchants of lower class to have a vestibulum like feature in their domus. For them, it acted as a separating hallway from the shops at the front of the domus and the family quarters deeper within. The typical vestibulum shaped the visitors’ view into the domus and acted to highlight the atrium, impluvium and elements further into the house such as the tablinum and peristylium.

Fauces

Fauces generally refers to the two narrow corridors situated on either side of the tablinum that leads between the atrium and the peristylium. Although the tablinum characteristically had an open window into the peristylium, it was not intended to be a general passage. Some will also refer to the vestibulum as a form of fauces given its form as a narrow hallway.
